Intro

An Appetite for Extraction

Kai AXL is a deep-tech venture based in Kaneohe, Hawaii, reimagining the relationship between industrial recovery and environmental stewardship. We don’t just process resources; we "tune" into them. By combining electrochemical precision with circular economic principles, we are securing the clean energy supply chain while protecting our most precious local resource: fresh water.

Mission

A Commitment to Conservation

Traditional desalination and mining are often at odds with the environment. Our mission is to prove they don't have to be. Through Zero-Liquid-Discharge (ZLD) technology, Kai AXL transforms "waste" into wealth. We are dedicated to providing Hawaii and the Pacific with a resilient, localized source of critical minerals and fresh water—eliminating the land footprint and the environmental cost of traditional extraction.

Minerals & Water

Dual-Stream Security

We treat the ocean as a comprehensive resource library, not a waste bin.

  • Battery-Grade Minerals: Our process targets high-value Lithium and Magnesium, essential for the global transition to renewable energy.
  • Fresh Water: By harvesting the minerals that make desalination brine "toxic," we produce agriculture-grade fresh water as a primary byproduct.
  • Local Co-Products: Our modular pods are designed to yield high-purity industrial Oxygen and gourmet-grade sea salt, creating a truly circular local economy.

Technology

The AXL Nucleolus™

At the core of Kai AXL is a proprietary, frequency-selective electrochemical reactor. Unlike traditional membranes that clog or fail, our system uses "ion resonance" to selectively target and recover specific elements from the water column.

  • Low Energy: Optimized for integration with renewable energy grids.
  • Modular: Scalable "pods" designed for rapid deployment in littoral environments.
  • Validated: Built on a foundation of UCLA Bio-Sciences and evolving through Chemical Engineering at Stanford University; currently moving from validated theory to localized field testing in Kaneohe Bay.
